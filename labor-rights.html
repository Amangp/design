Labor Rights & Fair Wages

Understanding your rights to fair compensation, proper classification, reasonable hours, and safe working conditions

Know Your Labor Rights

Every worker deserves fair treatment and compensation for their labor. Unfortunately, wage theft, misclassification, and violations of labor laws remain widespread problems affecting millions of workers across industries.

At Law & Order Workplace Rights Advocates, we help employees understand their labor rights, identify violations, and take effective action to recover unpaid wages and secure proper working conditions.

Whether you're dealing with unpaid overtime, minimum wage violations, or misclassification as an independent contractor, our resources and expertise can help you navigate these complex issues.

Your Essential Labor Rights

Federal and state laws provide important protections for workers. Understanding these rights is the first step to ensuring fair treatment:

Minimum Wage

You are entitled to receive at least the federal minimum wage ($15.50/hour as of 2025) or your state's minimum wage, whichever is higher. Some localities have established their own higher minimum wages.

This applies to most workers, regardless of whether you are paid hourly, by salary, or by piece rate.

Overtime Pay

Non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay of at least 1.5 times their regular rate for all hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek.

Some states provide additional overtime protections, such as daily overtime for work beyond 8 hours in a day.

Proper Classification

Workers must be properly classified as either employees or independent contractors. This classification affects tax withholding, benefits eligibility, and workplace protections.

Misclassification often deprives workers of overtime pay, unemployment insurance, and other benefits.

Break & Meal Periods

While federal law doesn't require break periods, many states mandate rest breaks and/or meal periods. When provided, short breaks (5-20 minutes) must generally be paid.

Meal breaks (typically 30+ minutes) may be unpaid if employees are completely relieved of duties.

Timely Payment

You have the right to receive your wages on regular paydays. Most states have laws specifying maximum intervals between paydays and final paycheck requirements.

Unauthorized deductions from your wages are generally prohibited.

Equal Pay

The Equal Pay Act and other laws prohibit wage discrimination based on sex for substantially equal work requiring equal skill, effort, and responsibility.

Some states have expanded equal pay protections to cover additional protected characteristics.

Recognizing Wage Theft

Wage theft occurs when employers fail to pay workers the full wages they've legally earned. This common workplace violation takes many forms:

Common Types of Wage Theft

Unpaid Overtime

Not paying for work over 40 hours per week, or misclassifying workers as "exempt" to avoid overtime requirements.

Off-the-Clock Work

Requiring work before/after shifts, during breaks, or from home without compensation.

Minimum Wage Violations

Paying less than the legal minimum wage, including for tipped workers when tips don't reach minimum wage.

Illegal Deductions

Taking unauthorized deductions that drop pay below minimum wage or withholding final paychecks.

Misclassification

Improperly classifying employees as independent contractors to avoid paying benefits and overtime.

Tip Theft

Managers or employers taking a portion of tips, or inappropriate tip pooling arrangements.

Red flags to watch for: Rounded time records, unpaid training or meetings, "off-the-clock" requests, payments made in cash without records, or being told "that's just how the industry works."

How to Address Wage & Labor Violations

If you believe your labor rights have been violated, follow these steps:

Document Everything

Keep detailed records of your hours worked, pay stubs, any communications about wages, and any instances of suspected violations.

Calculate Unpaid Wages

Determine how much you are owed by calculating your correct wages and comparing to what you received. Use our calculator below for assistance.

Address With Employer

Consider raising the issue with your supervisor, HR department, or payroll. Make your request in writing and keep copies of all communications.

File a Complaint

If direct resolution fails, file a wage claim with your state labor agency or the U.S. Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division.

Consult With an Advocate

Workplace rights advocates can help evaluate your case, navigate the complaint process, and discuss potential legal action if necessary.
