Workplace Discrimination

Understanding, recognizing, and addressing discrimination in the workplace to create more equitable environments for all employees.

Types of Workplace Discrimination

Workplace discrimination occurs when an employee or job applicant is treated unfavorably because of who they are. Learn about the common forms of discrimination and your rights:

Race & Ethnicity Discrimination

Treating an employee unfavorably because of race, color, or ethnic background. This includes:

  • Racial slurs or offensive remarks
  • Denial of opportunities due to race
  • Unequal enforcement of policies
  • Caste-based discrimination

Gender Discrimination

Treating employees differently based on gender, gender identity, or expression. Examples include:

  • Unequal pay for equal work
  • Gender-based promotion practices
  • Discriminatory hiring
  • Pregnancy discrimination

Age Discrimination

Treating workers aged 40 or older unfavorably due to age. Signs include:

  • Age-based layoffs or forced retirement
  • Age-specific job ads or requirements
  • Dismissive comments about age
  • Passing over qualified older workers

Disability Discrimination

Unfair treatment of qualified individuals with disabilities. This includes:

  • Failure to provide reasonable accommodations
  • Harassment based on disability
  • Disability-related inquiries before job offers
  • Segregation of workers with disabilities

Religion & Belief Discrimination

Unfavorable treatment based on religious beliefs or practices. Look for:

  • Denial of reasonable religious accommodations
  • Mandatory religious activities
  • Exclusion based on faith
  • Religious harassment

Other Protected Classes

Additional forms of discriminatory treatment include:

  • Sexual orientation discrimination
  • National origin discrimination
  • Military/veteran status discrimination
  • Genetic information discrimination

Recognizing Discrimination

Discrimination can be subtle or overt. Here are key indicators to help you identify potential discrimination:

Disparate Treatment

When similarly situated employees are treated differently based on protected characteristics.

Example: Two employees with similar qualifications and experience apply for the same promotion, but only the employee from the majority group is interviewed or considered.

Hostile Work Environment

When offensive conduct based on protected characteristics is severe or pervasive enough to create an intimidating workplace.

Example: Regular jokes, slurs, or derogatory comments targeting employees of a specific religion, with management failing to address the behavior despite complaints.

Systemic Patterns

When policies or practices disproportionately impact a protected group despite appearing neutral.

Example: A company policy requiring all employees to work specific religious holidays without exceptions, which adversely affects those who observe those holidays.

Retaliation

When an employer takes adverse action against an employee for reporting discrimination or participating in a discrimination investigation.

Example: An employee who files a discrimination complaint suddenly receives negative performance reviews, reduced hours, or is excluded from meetings.

Documenting Discrimination

Proper documentation is crucial when addressing workplace discrimination. Follow these steps to create a strong record:

1

Keep a Detailed Journal

Record incidents with dates, times, locations, people involved, witnesses, and exactly what was said or done. Note how the treatment differs from that of other employees.

2

Save Communications

Preserve emails, messages, letters, or notes that demonstrate discriminatory treatment. Forward work emails to a personal account if appropriate.

3

Obtain Company Policies

Get copies of relevant workplace policies, employee handbooks, and any communications regarding discrimination reporting procedures.

4

Report Formally

Follow your company's procedure for reporting discrimination. Keep copies of all complaints filed and responses received.

5

Track Performance

Maintain records of your performance, including reviews, achievements, and any recognition. This can help counter false claims of poor performance.
